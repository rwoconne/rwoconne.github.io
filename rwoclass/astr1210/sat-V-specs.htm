SATURN V TRIVIA -- DID YOU KNOW?

  • Because of the supercold propellants in its tanks, the Saturn V gained an additional 1,400 pounds of weight in ice forming on the skin of the propellant tanks before liftoff. It was almost all shed from the vehicle as the Saturn V's F-1 engines ignited and built up thrust before liftoff.
  • During each launch, the Saturn V would generate eighty-five times as much power as the Hoover Dam.
  • The combined power of the first two stages generated enough energy to supply all of New York City for 1 hour and 15 minutes.
  • Contrary to popular belief, clouds do not form inside the Vehicle Assembly Building (VAB). The foundation pilings of the VAB are over 200 feet below the surface.
  • The explosive potential of a fully-fueled Saturn V was the equivalent of a small nuclear weapon. With the exception of emergency fire-rescue crews stationed about 1 kilometer (3280 feet) from the pad, the nearest spectators were 3-1/2 miles (5.6 km) away, near the Vehicle Assembly Building (VAB).
  • Fuel economy of the Saturn V was atrocious -- about a few inches to the gallon!
  • General Motors' Saturn line of cars is named after the Saturn launch vehicles. (On the other hand, my mother's Saturn SL1 will nearly fit inside the bell of a single F-1 engine, and gets MUCH better gas mileage <grin>! -- CEC)
  • If you laid the Saturn V on its side, its overall length exceeds the length of a football or soccer field. The height of the vehicle is equivalent to a 36-story building.
  • During the launch of SA-512 (Apollo 17), the glow its fiery liftoff and the exhaust plume were visible as far north as Cape Hatteras, North Carolina and as far south as Cuba. As SA-512 built up thrust and lifted off from the pad many described the launch as a second sunrise, even tricking the photocells on nearby street lamps into turning off. The actual, official sunrise was only but a few hours away.
  • A fully-fueled Saturn V stood 7-7/8 inches (20 cm) shorter than when unfueled, due to settling caused by the weight of the propellants.
  • During the launch of Apollo 4, sensitive instruments in New York City picked up the shock wave of the liftoff of the Saturn V.

NOMENCLATURE OF SATURN VEHICLES

All Saturn launch vehicles and stages were identified with Roman numerals. The correct designations are used throughout these pages. The Saturn vehicles were pronounced in NASA jargon Saturn-five, Saturn-one-B and Saturn-one, while the stages were pronounced S-one-C, S-two, and S-four-B. Depending on which center issued the documents, even the vehicle's serial number designation was different: Marshall Space Flight Center used the designation SA (for Saturn-Apollo) followed by the serial number for all Saturn vehicles, while the Manned Spacecraft Center (now Johnson Space Center) used the designation AS (for Apollo-Saturn) followed by the serial number in their documents. Which one is correct? Actually, both of them are, though Marshall designations are more correct.
